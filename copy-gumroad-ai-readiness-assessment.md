# Is Your Business Ready for AI?
## A 5-Minute Self-Assessment for E-Commerce Operations Teams
*By Artem Stepanenko — SCAIT*

---

You run supply chain operations — sourcing, inventory, 3PLs, Amazon. You've heard AI can automate a lot of this. But can it actually help *your* business, right now? This assessment tells you where you stand. Answer 15 questions, get a score, and leave with a clear next step — not a vague recommendation, but a specific action you can take this week.

## How to Score

For each question, pick the answer that best describes your current situation:

- **0** — No / Not yet / We don't do this
- **1** — Sometimes / Partially / Inconsistently
- **2** — Yes / Always / This is standard practice

Write your score next to each question. Add them up at the end.

## Section 1: Data & Visibility
*Does your business have the data AI needs to work with?*

AI doesn't create data — it works with the data you already have. If your data is scattered, incomplete, or lives in someone's head, AI can't help much yet. These questions check whether your data foundation is solid.

---

**Q1. Do you have a single source of truth for inventory levels across all SKUs and locations?**

In other words: if someone asks "how many units of Product X do we have right now, across all warehouses and Amazon FBA?" — can you answer in under 60 seconds without calling anyone?

- 0 — No, I'd need to check multiple places or ask someone
- 1 — Mostly, but some SKUs or locations are missing
- 2 — Yes, one place, always up to date

**Q2. Do you receive and store 3PL invoices in a structured format (spreadsheet or system), not just as PDF emails?**

If your 3PL sends you a PDF invoice every month and it goes straight to accounts payable without any line-by-line review — that's a 0.

- 0 — No, invoices come in as PDFs and we pay them
- 1 — We review them manually but don't store data in a structured way
- 2 — Yes, invoice line items are recorded in a spreadsheet or system

**Q3. Can you pull 90 days of sales history for any SKU in under 5 minutes, without asking another team member?**

This is about access and speed. If getting sales data requires a Slack message, a BI request, or an export from Seller Central that only one person knows how to run — that's a gap.

- 0 — No, it takes more than a day or requires someone else
- 1 — Yes, but it's slow or requires several manual steps
- 2 — Yes, I can do it myself in under 5 minutes

**Q4. Do you know your current sell-through rate and days-of-cover for your top 10 SKUs right now (not last week, not last month)?**

Days-of-cover = units in stock ÷ average daily sales. If you don't know this number off the top of your head or from a live dashboard, you're flying partially blind.

- 0 — No, we don't track this regularly
- 1 — We check it monthly or only for certain SKUs
- 2 — Yes, we track it weekly or in real time for all key SKUs

**Score: ___**

---

**Q5. Do you have a record of your 3PL's rate card, and do you check invoices against it?**

Your 3PL charges for storage, inbound, outbound, labeling, special handling. The rate card is the contract. If you can't say with confidence what the correct rate is for each service — and compare it against what you're actually billed — overcharges go unnoticed.

- 0 — I don't have the rate card or it's not current
- 1 — I have it but we don't systematically check invoices against it
- 2 — Yes, rate card is current and we verify charges against it

## Section 2: Process Maturity
*Are your operations structured enough to automate?*

AI automates processes — not chaos. If your workflow is "we figure it out as we go," automation will just make the chaos faster. These questions check whether you have repeatable processes AI can take over.

---

**Q6. Do you have a documented reorder process — a clear trigger point, a defined lead time, and a standard minimum order quantity for your main SKUs?**

Not in your head. Documented. Written down somewhere your team can follow without calling you.

- 0 — No, reorder decisions are made case by case
- 1 — We have informal rules but they're not written down
- 2 — Yes, documented reorder points and lead times for main SKUs

**Score: ___**

---

**Q7. When a supplier sends you a quote or invoice, do you follow a consistent review process — checking quantities, pricing, lead times against a previous version?**

Or does it go straight to approval because you're busy and you trust them?

- 0 — No standard review, we read it and approve
- 1 — We check the big numbers but not systematically
- 2 — Yes, we compare against previous quotes and a rate reference

**Score: ___**

---

**Q8. Do you run a weekly or bi-weekly inventory review — scheduled, with a defined output (reorder list, alerts, decisions)?**

A meeting where someone looks at a spreadsheet and says "looks OK" is not a structured review. A defined output means: at the end of this review, we have a list of actions.

- 0 — No regular review, we react to stockouts
- 1 — We review occasionally but without a standard output
- 2 — Yes, scheduled review with a defined output (actions, reorder list)

**Score: ___**

**Q9. Can you trace why a stockout or overstock happened — root cause, not just "sales were higher than expected"?**

Root cause means: which data signal was wrong, which process failed, and when did it first become visible? If you can answer that question for your last stockout, your process has some structure. If not, stockouts are still a surprise.

- 0 — No, we just know it happened and try to fix it
- 1 — We can partly explain it but not trace back to a specific failure
- 2 — Yes, we can identify the data gap or process failure that caused it

**Score: ___**

**Q10. Do you have a standard way of logging and resolving supplier issues — late shipments, quality failures, wrong quantities?**

A standard way means: there's a place where it's recorded, someone is responsible, and there's a follow-up step. Not "we sent them a message on WhatsApp."

- 0 — No, we handle issues as they come, nothing is logged
- 1 — We email them and follow up, but it's not tracked
- 2 — Yes, issues are logged with outcomes tracked

## Scoring Guide

| Score | Label | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| 0–10 | Not Ready Yet | The foundation needs work before AI can help |
| 11–20 | Getting There | You have enough structure to start — strategically |
| 21–30 | Ready to Automate | You're leaving real money on the table right now |

---

### Not Ready Yet (0–10)

Your operations are running on instinct and manual effort — and that's not unusual at this stage. The problem is that AI amplifies what's already there. If processes are inconsistent and data is scattered, automation will just lock in the chaos. The good news: the gaps are fixable, and fixing them doesn't require software. It requires decisions.

Most companies at this score are stuck in the same three places: no single inventory source of truth, no structured invoice review, and no ownership clarity. Fix those three things and your score jumps 6–8 points.

---

### Getting There (11–20)

You have real structure in place — some processes are documented, someone owns operations, and you're not completely flying blind. What's holding you back is consistency and coverage. Some workflows work well; others still rely on memory, trust, and manual follow-up.

This is actually the most actionable score. You don't need to rebuild from scratch — you need to identify the one or two highest-ROI gaps and plug them. The companies that make the jump from this range to automation do it by picking one workflow (usually invoices or inventory monitoring) and building a repeatable system around it.

---

### Ready to Automate (21–30)

Your operations are structured, your data is reasonably clean, and your team has the tools and willingness to work differently. That means you're ready to automate — and if you haven't started, you're leaving money on the table every month.

At this score, the typical first-year impact of AI workflows is $15,000–$90,000 in recovered costs and avoided losses, depending on your scale. The biggest wins are usually 3PL invoice recovery (overcharges you've been paying for months), stockout prevention (losing rank on Amazon is expensive), and demand monitoring (catching shifts 10–14 days earlier than your current process).
