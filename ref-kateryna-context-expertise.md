## 2. Who She Works With

### Two current client profiles:

**Profile A — Women 30–45 in crisis**  
Already in a breaking point. Trying to change something. Don't know how to be with their husbands, children, themselves, their career. This was the larger group until recently.

**Profile B — Girls 20–25**  
Completely different questions. Mostly connected to internal self-worth and self-sufficiency — which they often don't have.

### Developing:

**Profile C — Men 35+**  
Outwardly realized — career, family, money. Internally — tired and confused. "Everything seems fine but something is off. Everything's fine but something feels past." This profile she wants to build intentionally.

### Who is NOT her client:
- Minors
- Military (PTSD)
- Severe clinical disorders (autism, ADHD)
- Young guys without life experience — "not interesting for me to work with"

### Most interesting audience:
Girls 20-something AND adults 40–50. Both ends of the spectrum.

## 4. The Client's Language

### What they say at the first session:
"I don't know where to start."  
Her response: "There are no rules. You can start from wherever, from whomever you want to review. You don't need words. You don't need to formulate correctly. If you need to cry — please. I'm not a teacher. I don't grade here. There's no right or wrong, no good or bad. We met — please start. From wherever you want, from whatever moment you want."

### Most common pattern (said AS A CONCLUSION after first meeting, not at start):
**"I undervalue myself. I don't hear myself."**  
This is what they arrive at — not what they say when they walk in. They all say different things. It's the root they reach together.

### Barriers to seeing a psychologist (her hierarchy):
1. **Money** — the first obstacle
2. **Trust** — the second
3. **Bad previous experience** — with incompetent colleagues, or their acquaintances had a bad experience. "There are a lot of psychologists. Every specialist is different."

### About fear of the first session:
Clients who come by recommendation usually don't come with fear. They come in a "breaking point" — they want to resolve the issue. Nervous clients were rare — so rare she doesn't remember them.

## 5. Transformation — What Actually Changes

### How the journey looks:
Client arrives completely broken. Everything is falling apart — usually from conflicts. With husband, children, self. Life changed but "not theoretically enough."

"Everyone wants quick results. They come a week or two later dissatisfied — no results yet. It turns out they've done a lot. It's just that we demand too much from ourselves."

### What she tells every client:
**"Please don't measure yourself and your life in frames of a week or a month. Look at six months — there it will all be unfairly visible."**

Six months doesn't mean working every week for six months. Sometimes they work toward maintenance — a recap once a month.

### The hair growth analogy:
"It's like wanting your hair to grow. You can check every single day — you'll never see it growing. But if you step back after six months, you'll see 30 cm of growth. It's the same."

### The key sign progress happened:
**Clients stop self-blaming.**  
"This is our national pastime — blaming yourself for everything. They come in, they do it. They heal, they stop doing it."

### What clients say when they finish:
**"I was — and I'm fine in my life."**

### How she sees her role in a session:
"I give exactly what I can give. The person takes exactly as much as they can take. Every session for the person who comes to me is definitely useful. I don't perceive sessions as good or bad."

## 6. Anonymous Cases

### Case 1 — Financial floor and self-worth
Client came with depression and high anxiety. Husband earned well; she earned less. On St. Nicholas Day he gave her an iPhone — she couldn't give back a gift at the same level. She was very anxious, family demanded she "create comfort," she forgot to pay bills on time, forgot appointments. She said to herself constantly: "I just don't care."

Then husband gave her a new car. She accepted it. She started doing great gifts for family, caring for her loved ones beautifully — but now she recognized her contribution. She realized it. She wrote to Kateryna that she'd broken her financial ceiling. Now she's very happy.

**"Six months ago, look at who she was. She didn't believe her life could continue. Now she's very happy."**

### Case 2 — Betrayal, legal battle, finding herself
Client came with complex situation. They worked for three years, through ATO. Her husband was military — suspicions of something, then it turned out he had cheated the last year and a half. He wanted to take the apartment and car (apartment bought by her parents, but ex-husband had a very good lawyer — brother of the former husband). Result: he got the car. Her son gave her a new car. He wanted money for the apartment — she paid.

She found her love. Now very happy. She'd seen how many people truly surrounded and supported her — even the ex-husband's wife supported her. She broke her financial ceiling too.

**"I don't accept the format of 'things went wrong or right.' I give what I can at that session. The result depends on the person's responsibility."**

## 7. Her Position and Values

### What she believes in (core):
- **"You can live better."**
- **"Suffering is not mandatory."**
- **"Being a victim your whole life is not your destiny."**
- **"Everything can change."**
- **"You can pull yourself out. Can't do it alone — go get help."**
- **"Trauma can stop hurting. You don't have to mourn your most painful experience for your whole life."**

### What she'd change in society:
She'd want every person to ask themselves one question, always:  
**"What do I want right now?"**  
Not social media. Not targeted ads. What do I ACTUALLY want right now. "If everyone answered that honestly, I don't know — maybe we'd live differently."

### What she'd never say to a client:
"That they're guilty. Or that they deserved what they lived through."  
"I won't say anything that could traumatize. For what? For whom? People who come to me have already lived through something. They come for something different."

### About other psychologists on social media:
"Honestly — nothing annoys me. Everyone does what they want. I only answer for myself."

### What people misunderstand about therapy:
"People who actually come to therapy already understand everything correctly. Those who misunderstand are those who heard gossip and weren't really interested. Regular people just need to come. Those who misunderstand — they come and ask, get the answer, and go."

## 11. Pricing and Sales

### Her stance on pricing:
"I never explained, never justified. But I have the right to. A consultation can cost as much as needed. This is not a store. Not a basic necessity."

### Who can afford the 30,000 package:
Two portraits:
1. Woman with good job or business of her own
2. Woman with a partner who earns well and whose goal is to earn more herself / enjoy life / stop serving everyone

More broadly: **a person who's already tired of living by others' interests and is ready to change consistently — not just "come once."**

### Difference between a 4,000 client and a 30,000 client:
**"It's not about 46,000 balance difference. It's about mentality."**  
"The scale of personality is different. The self-permission is different. If a person can theoretically pay 50k, that means their ceiling isn't 20–30–40k/month. They think cleaner. They live cleaner. They're not stuck. The perception of the world in them is fundamentally different."

### When someone says "too expensive":
She uses the gym analogy:  
**"What do you get from a trainer for a year vs. what you get alone? Alone for a year — nothing. You need to be regular. Coming once for 40 minutes — fine, but that's definitely not enough. You can come once a month to a psychologist, work once a month and spend years untangling the same question. Want faster — go with someone. With a trainer, mentor, psychologist. Don't stay alone — you have blind spots everywhere."**

### Clients who invested seriously:
**"All clients who invested money were satisfied. All of them. They said it was exactly right, correctly valued. Even if it wasn't their last money."**
