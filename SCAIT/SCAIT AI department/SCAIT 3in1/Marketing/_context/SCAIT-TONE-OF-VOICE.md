## Voice Pillars

### 1. Operator-to-Operator

Talk like a peer who's been in the same situation — not a consultant explaining theory.

| Wrong | Right |
|-------|-------|
| "Supply chain inefficiencies can result in significant financial losses." | "We missed a $2,914 price hike because it was buried in paragraph three of a supplier email." |
| "Our solution helps streamline your procurement communications." | "I read supplier emails for you, flag what matters, and draft the reply." |
| "Leverage AI to optimize your supply chain operations." | "Your 3PL invoices are wrong about 50% of the time under manual review. Here's how to catch the other half." |

### 2. Numbers Over Claims

Every statement should be anchored to a specific number, case, or outcome.

- Not "significant savings" → **$91,800 recovered in 8 months**
- Not "faster processing" → **45 minutes → 10 seconds to find any inspection report**
- Not "early warning system" → **stockout signals visible 14 days before the event in data you already have**

Rule: If you can't attach a number, don't make the claim.

### 3. Direct and Economical

No filler. No preamble. No hedge words.

- Cut: "It's worth noting that…", "One thing to consider is…", "In many cases…"
- Cut: "Streamline", "Leverage", "Robust", "Seamless", "End-to-end", "Best-in-class"
- Keep: Short sentences. Active voice. The point first, the explanation after.

### 4. Honest About What This Is

SCAIT is a manual + AI service in Phase 1. Not enterprise software. Not a SaaS platform. Don't pretend otherwise.

- Don't say: "Our platform automatically processes…"
- Do say: "I process your supplier emails using AI and deliver results within 4 hours."

This honesty builds trust. The ICP values directness more than polish.

### 5. Calm Confidence

No urgency theater. No fear-mongering. No "act now before it's too late."

Present facts. Let the math speak. If the ROI is real ($2,914 overcharge on one $47K order), the prospect will feel the urgency themselves.

## Tone by Channel

### LinkedIn Posts

- Conversational, first-person, past-tense story structure
- Start with a specific number or incident — not a question or rhetorical hook
- End with a clear takeaway, not a CTA
- Vocabulary: SC practitioner talking to SC practitioners
- Length: 150–250 words for story posts; 50–100 for observations

**Example opening (right):**
> "A supplier raised prices by 6.2% on a $47,000 order. The client didn't notice. Here's how that happens."

**Example opening (wrong):**
> "Are you leaving money on the table with your supplier communications? 🚀"

---

### Cold DMs / Outreach

- No generic compliments
- Open with something specific to the prospect (their post, their product category, their visible pain)
- Lead with the lead magnet — never pitch a call as the first ask
- 2–4 sentences max for first message
- Tone: peer-to-peer, not salesperson-to-prospect

---

### Sales Call / Demo

- Consultant frame: "Let me show you what I found" — not "let me show you what we sell"
- Ask questions, pause, don't fill silence
- ROI math presented calmly, not dramatically
- Close by referencing the specific pain the prospect mentioned — not a generic script

---

### Email Delivery to Clients

- Functional. No pleasantries.
- Subject: `SCAI Reply Ready — [Supplier Name] — [YYYY-MM-DD]`
- One sentence of context max, then the deliverable
- Always include a sheet link

---

### Product Descriptions / Website

- Problem-first structure: what breaks, why it breaks, what SCAIT does about it
- Every product has a real number attached
- No feature lists without outcomes
- Avoid passive voice

---

## Language Rules

### Always

- Use specific numbers (dollars, minutes, percentages, days)
- Name the product abbreviation after introduction: Supplier Communication AI (SCAI)
- Write the way a smart operator talks — not the way a startup markets itself
- Use "brand owner", "SC team", "forwarder", "3PL", "supplier" — the vocabulary the ICP uses

### Never

- "Leverage" (use "use")
- "Streamline" (say what specifically gets faster/better)
- "Robust" (describe the actual capability)
- "AI-powered" as a standalone selling point (AI is the method, not the value)
- Exclamation points in B2B copy
- Emojis in sales messages (LinkedIn posts: max one, functional, not decorative)
- Generic claims without numbers

---

## Proof-First Principle

SCAIT content always moves from evidence to conclusion — not from claim to justification.

**Structure:**
1. Here's what happened (specific case or stat)
2. Here's why it happened (systemic cause)
3. Here's what SCAIT does about it
4. Here's the number it's worth

Not: "SCAIT catches overcharges → for example, $91,800."  
Yes: "$91,800 in overcharges over 8 months. Zone 6 billed under Zone 4 contract. Undetected because manual review catches ~50% of 3PL invoice errors. SC 3PL Guard catches the rest."

## Red Lines

These things are never done in SCAIT communications:

1. **No invented ROI.** Every number is real and verified. If unsure — don't publish.
2. **No "AI will replace your team."** SCAIT augments SC professionals, it doesn't replace them.
3. **No enterprise positioning before it's earned.** SCAIT is a founder-built service. Lean into it.
4. **No urgency manipulation.** "Only 3 spots left" or "prices going up Friday" — never.
5. **No comparison attacks.** Never name competitors and tear them down. Show the ROI and let it stand.
