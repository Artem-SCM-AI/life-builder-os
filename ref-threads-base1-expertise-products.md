## 7 Core Content Topics

### Topic 1 — The True Cost of Manual Job Search
The average professional spends 8–12 hours/week searching manually.
That's 400–600 hours/year. One full month of 40-hour work weeks. For a process that can be automated.
Key angle: Time is the invisible price you pay. Most people don't count it.

### Topic 2 — Job Search Is a Numbers Game (And You're Losing)
The first to apply wins. Most job postings get 70% of applications within the first 24 hours.
If you're checking manually once a day, you're already late.
Key angle: Speed of application correlates directly with interview rate. Automation fixes the speed problem.

### Topic 3 — Why Tracking 20+ Applications in Your Head Doesn't Work
People forget which version of their resume they sent. Forget to follow up. Lose track of status.
The mental load of tracking is itself exhausting.
Key angle: A structured pipeline (automated) replaces a spreadsheet you never update.

### Topic 4 — AI vs. Classic Automation for Job Search
Not every job search problem needs AI. Some just need rules.
"If posted today AND matches my role AND salary above X → add to list" — that's a rule, not AI.
Key angle: Knowing which tool to use saves you from over-engineering a simple problem.

### Topic 5 — The "I Don't Want My Boss to Know I'm Looking" Problem
80% of working professionals who are job searching are doing it secretly.
They can't use company email. They check job boards on their phone during lunch.
They're terrified of a LinkedIn activity update showing "Open to Work."
Key angle: Automation runs silently on your personal Mac. No company email. No trace.

### Topic 6 — From 0 to First Interview: What the Automated Process Looks Like
Walk through the actual daily experience: you sleep, the agent searches, you wake up to a ranked list.
Show the Notion pipeline. Show the score. Show the "Why This Job" note the agent writes.
Key angle: Make it concrete. Not "automation" — but "8 new jobs in your pipeline this morning."

### Topic 7 — The Human Side: What You Do With the Time You Get Back
You didn't get into this field to spend 10 hours/week looking for jobs.
What do you do when those 10 hours come back?
Spend time with your family. Sleep. Work on your actual skills. Apply to better jobs with more care.
Key angle: The emotional/human angle. Automation isn't about laziness — it's about spending energy where it matters.
