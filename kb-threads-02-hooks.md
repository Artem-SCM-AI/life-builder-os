# Knowledge Base 2 — Hooks & Opening Lines
*Distilled from 40 research sources. For Claude Projects — Threads Content Machine.*

---

## Why Hooks Are Everything

On Threads, you have 2 seconds to stop the scroll. The entire job of the first line is to make someone pause.

If the hook fails → the post doesn't exist. No engagement, no distribution.
If the hook works → the algorithm gives it a chance.

**Rule:** Write the hook LAST. Write the body first, then reverse-engineer the best opening line.

---

## The 3-Step Hook Formula

Every high-performing hook has three layers:

**Step 1 — Context Lean**
Immediately clear what the post is about. Self-select in or out. Then add one of:
- Common ground with reader
- A pain point or benefit they already feel
- A number that frames the scale
- A metaphor that simplifies complexity

**Step 2 — Scroll-Stop Interjection**
One short sentence built on contrast: "but," "except," "the problem is," "and most people don't know."
Not the payoff yet — just the moment that creates suspension.

**Step 3 — Contrarian Snapback**
The sentence that lands in the opposite direction of the initial lean.
The bigger the gap between lean and snap, the stronger the hook.

**Example:**
- Lean: "You're applying to the right jobs."
- Interjection: "But here's what's costing you interviews."
- Snapback: "It has nothing to do with your resume."

## 8 Hook Formulas That Work on Threads

**Formula 1 — The Number**
State a specific, surprising number in the first line.
> "You spend 10 hours a week searching for jobs manually."
> "The first 70% of applications arrive within 24 hours of a job posting."
> "Most people are tracking 20+ applications in their head. Zero chance."

**Formula 2 — The Contradiction**
State something that seems wrong but is true.
> "You're applying to the right jobs. You're just applying too late."
> "Your resume isn't the problem. Your timing is."
> "Job searching isn't about quality. It's about speed."

**Formula 3 — The Uncomfortable Truth**
Say the thing no one wants to say directly.
> "Your job search is a second job. You don't get paid for it."
> "Most job boards show you jobs that are already filled."
> "You're not bad at job searching. You're doing it manually in a market that rewards automation."

**Formula 4 — The Direct Address**
Name the exact person, role, or situation.
> "IT professional in a toxic job: this is why you haven't left yet."
> "Procurement manager: your job search looks like a second procurement project."
> "If you've been quietly job searching while employed — this is for you."

**Formula 5 — The "I did this" story opener**
First person, past tense, specific.
> "I searched for jobs manually for 3 months. Here's what I learned."
> "I automated my job search. Here's what changed."
> "I spent 12 hours a week on job boards. Then I stopped."

**Formula 6 — The Short List Tease**
Promise a specific list in the body.
> "3 reasons your job search is taking longer than it should."
> "The 2 things that determine whether you get an interview (hint: it's not the resume)."

**Formula 7 — The Question (use sparingly)**
Only works if the question is specific enough to be uncomfortable.
> "When's the last time a job found you?"
> "How many applications have you lost track of this month?"
Bad: "Have you ever struggled with job searching?" (too generic, no one stops)

**Formula 8 — The "What if" reframe**
> "What if your job search ran itself every morning?"
> "What if you woke up to a ranked list of the best jobs posted overnight?"

## Psychological Principles Behind High-Performing Hooks

**Pain > Benefit at the top**
People stop for pain faster than for gain. "You're losing 10 hours a week" stops faster than "You could save 10 hours a week."

**Specificity creates credibility**
"10 hours/week" beats "a lot of time."
"70% of applications arrive in the first 24 hours" beats "most people apply too late."
"$47 one-time setup" beats "affordable."

**Self-identification in the first 3 words**
If the reader can recognize themselves in the first line, they read on.
"IT professionals:" "Procurement managers:" "If you're quietly job searching:" — all work.

**Staccato sentences at the top**
Short. Sharp. Dense. Information per word is high.
Long warm-up sentences kill the scroll stop.

**Create a curiosity gap**
The hook should raise a question the reader can't answer without reading on.
The body resolves it. Never reveal the full answer in the hook.

## Hook Examples for Artem's Niche (Job Search Automation)

**For cold audience (pain awareness):**
- "You check LinkedIn three times a day. The job you want is gone by morning."
- "Most job seekers spend 5–15 hours a week searching manually. Every week."
- "Most job searches fail before the interview. Here's the real reason."
- "You're not too picky. You're just applying too late."
- "Your job search has a second job inside it. You're not getting paid for either."

**For warm audience (solution awareness):**
- "I automated my job search. Here's exactly what changed."
- "What a job search looks like when it runs itself every morning."
- "30 minutes of setup. Then it runs while you sleep."
- "The agent applied to 50 jobs for me while I was at work."

**For specific roles:**
- "IT professional in a role you hate: here's why you haven't left yet."
- "Procurement manager: your job search needs the same system as your procurement."
- "Realtor: you find deals for everyone else. Why is finding your next job so hard?"
- "If you don't want your employer to know you're looking — this runs silently on your Mac."

## Hooks to Avoid

- "Have you ever wondered..." — too soft, no scroll-stop
- "In today's world..." — generic opener, scroll past
- "I wanted to share something..." — buries the lead
- "As many of you know..." — corporate filler
- Any question that can be answered with "no" and scrolled past
- Starting with "I" unless the "I" immediately connects to their pain

## The First Comment Strategy

After publishing, immediately add a strong first comment that:
1. Gives a bonus insight not in the main post
2. OR asks a specific follow-up question
3. OR provides the link (if it was in the post body)

This seeds the reply chain, gives the algorithm an early engagement signal, and adds more value without bloating the main post.

Example post ends with: "Find out your number — link in bio."
First comment: "Most people I've shown this to are surprised the number is over 8 hours. What's yours?"
