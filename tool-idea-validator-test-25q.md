# Is This a Good Business Idea?
### 25-Question Validation Assessment

*Based on the frameworks used by Y Combinator, Strategyzer, and leading startup investors*

---

## How This Works

Think about one specific business idea.
Answer each question about that idea on a scale from 0 to 4.

**Scoring scale:**
- **0** — No / we have no evidence of this
- **1** — Possibly / we're assuming this
- **2** — Probably / some evidence
- **3** — Likely / solid evidence
- **4** — Confirmed / tested with real people or data

The difference between 0 and 4 is not opinion. It's evidence.

## Your Result

| Score | Verdict | What it means |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| **0–35** | Weak idea | Major gaps in problem clarity, market, or revenue model. Rethink before investing time or money. The gap is not execution — it's fundamentals. |
| **36–55** | Needs validation | Some promising elements, but critical questions are still assumptions. Don't build yet. Spend 2 weeks talking to real customers first. |
| **56–75** | Promising | Solid foundation. The direction is right. Validate aggressively with real customers — price, willingness to pay, and first 10 leads — before building anything significant. |
| **76–100** | Strong idea | You have the key elements in place. The risk now is over-planning instead of launching. Start selling this week — the best validation is a paying customer. |

## How to Read Your Block Scores

**Block 1 (Problem) is the foundation.**
42% of startups fail because they built something nobody needed. If your Block 1 score is below 12, stop and do customer discovery before anything else. You are working on assumptions, not a business.

**Block 2 (Market) determines the ceiling.**
A brilliant solution to a small problem is a lifestyle business at best. Know your numbers: TAM, who pays, and how you reach them. "Everyone" is not a market.

**Block 3 (Revenue) is the most ignored block.**
Most first-time founders treat revenue as something that comes after the product is built. The best indicator of a viable business is whether someone pays you before the product is finished. Score this ruthlessly.

**Block 4 (Competition) is misunderstood.**
No competition = no market. Too much dominant competition = no room. The sweet spot: active competitors with obvious weaknesses. And "better" is not a differentiator — that's what everyone says.

**Block 5 (Launch) is where most ideas die.**
Not from failure — from delay. If you can't describe your first 10 customers by name or specific profile, you don't have a go-to-market — you have a hope. Specificity is everything.

---

## The Three Most Dangerous Answers

**"Everyone could be my customer."**
No market = no business.

**"There's no competition."**
Either the problem doesn't exist, or you haven't looked hard enough.

**"I'll figure out monetization later."**
Revenue is not a detail. It is the business.
