# DMs That Deliver Your Resource And Open Real Conversations

Most people send the same DM to every commenter. LinkedIn flags it. Your account gets throttled. Your reach drops. And even when it doesn't get flagged, the same message to 200 people reads like a script. Because it is.
The fix is simple: rotate 5 variations. Same skeleton, different wording. Every DM should do 3 things — deliver the resource immediately, treat them like a human, and plant a hook for the follow-up question.
This is the exact rotation I run after every lead magnet post.

# The 5 Variations (Mix and Rotate)
**Variation 1 — The Direct Drop**
**Variation 2 — The Acknowledgment Open**
**Variation 3 — The Personal Touch**
**Variation 4 — The Soft Authority**
**Variation 5 — The Question First**

---

# DM Rotation Rules
## Don't send the same variation to 10 people in a row
Mix it up. If you delivered Variation 1 to your last 5 commenters, switch to Variation 3.
## Lowercase is fine
It's a DM, not a sales page. Casual reads as human.
## One exclamation point max. Zero is fine.
Every extra "!" makes you sound less like a real person.
## No corporate speak
"I'd love to explore synergies" — instant unsubscribe.
## Always send within 24 hours
After 24 hours, the post momentum is gone and they've forgotten why they commented.

# Scaling The Delivery
You can do this manually for the first 100–200 comments. After that, it gets painful fast.
There are tools that handle the delivery part automatically — LeadShark is the one I'd start with. It pulls every commenter from your post, sends your DM with the resource link, and rotates variations so LinkedIn doesn't flag the account.
That frees you up to focus on the only thing that actually books calls: the conversation after the DM.
Automate the delivery. Never automate the dialogue.
The DM that drops the link can be a tool. The reply when they say "thanks" should always be you. That's where your money is.

# Qualifying Every Commenter In 10 Seconds Or Less

Most people treat every commenter like a hot lead. They aren't. Some are competitors. Some are tire-kickers. Some are 19-year-olds on LinkedIn for the first time.
You don't have time to send a 5-message follow-up sequence to all of them. So before you spend energy on a conversation, scan their profile in 10 seconds and decide who's actually worth chasing.
This is the exact filter I run on every commenter.

---

# The 4-Signal Scan
Each signal is a yes/no. Score 0–4.

---

# What Each Score Means
4/4 — Hot lead
→ Treat like a priority
→ Personalize the delivery DM
→ Move them straight into the follow-up sequence
→ These are who you should be spending your time on
2–3/4 — Nurture
→ Run the standard DM rotation
→ Send the universal follow-up question
→ If they engage well, move them up the priority list
0–1/4 — Don't burn energy
→ Send the resource via Variation 5 (shortest)
→ Skip the follow-up sequence
→ Let the resource do the work — if they reach out, great
→ Move on

---

# Where to Look in 10 Seconds
→ Their headline (first thing on profile)
→ Their company name + size (right under headline)
→ Their last 1-2 posts or activity (top of profile)
→ Their comment text ("DMS" vs. "I've been struggling with this for 2 years")
That's it. Don't go deeper. The 10-second cap is the point.

---

# The Mistake Most People Make
They treat every comment like a hot lead. They send a personalized DM, do all the research, follow up 4 times… and the person was a college student who wanted free tips.
You'll book more calls by spending 80% of your energy on the 4/4s than spreading thin across 200 commenters.
The score isn't gatekeeping. It's leverage.

# The Follow-Up Sequence That Turns Thanks Into Booked Calls

Most conversations die at "thanks, you're welcome!" 
That's where the money leaks out.
90% of replies after the delivery DM are one of three things:
→ "Thanks!"
→ Silence
→ An actual question
This is the 4-message sequence that turns any of those into a calendar invite — without sounding like a desperate sales pitch.

---

# The Universal Follow-Up Question
After they receive the resource, this single question opens a real conversation no matter how they respond:
"out of curiosity — what's the actual reason you're looking into [topic] right now? is this a 'just looking around' thing or a 'we need to fix this' thing?"
## Why it works
→ It doesn't pitch
→ It forces a binary answer (browsing vs. buying)
→ "Out of curiosity" removes pressure — they don't feel sold to
→ If they're serious, it opens a real conversation
→ If they're not, it politely closes the loop and saves your time

---

# The 4-Message Sequence
## Message 1 — The Curiosity Open
Send immediately after they say "thanks"
`glad it's helpful!

quick question — are you trying to apply this 
to your own business or a client's?`
## Message 2 — The Diagnostic
After they answer
`got it.

what's working for you right now,
and what feels broken?`
## Message 3 — The Pivot
After they describe the problem
`yeah, that's the exact pattern I see most.

the fix is usually [1-line specific insight].

if it'd help, I can walk you through what 
I'd do specifically in your situation.
20 mins, no pitch. want me to send a link?`
## Message 4 — The Soft Close
When they say yes
`[calendar link]

pick whatever works. if you can send me your 
website / LinkedIn / quick context before the call,
I'll come in with specific recommendations 
instead of generic advice`

---

# The Critical Rule
Don't skip Message 2.
The diagnostic is what makes Message 3 land. If you pivot to "want to chat?" right after they say what they're working on, it feels like a pitch.
The diagnostic question makes them describe the problem in their own words. Now Message 3 is responding to THEIR problem — not pushing your offer.

---

# Timing Between Messages
→ Message 1: Same day, ideally within an hour of their reply
→ Message 2: After they reply (could be minutes, could be days)
→ Message 3: After they reply
→ Message 4: Within 5 mins of their yes (don't make them wait once they're warm)
Move at THEIR pace, not yours. If they go quiet for 3 days, don't double-text.

---

# When They Go Quiet
After 5 days of silence at any stage, send ONE re-engagement message:
`hey — circling back. did you get a chance 
to look through [resource]?

no pressure either way, just wanted to make 
sure you got it`
If still silent after that, drop them. They'll come back when they're ready.

# Soft CTAs That Book Calls Without Sounding Like A Pitch

Most CTAs sound like sales pitches because they ARE sales pitches.
"Want to hop on a call?" 
"Let me show you what we do." 
"I'd love to chat about your business."
All three sound like sales. None of them work.
The soft CTA flips the script. Instead of asking them to give you 30 minutes of their time, you offer 20 minutes of yours — solving THEIR problem.
This is the exact structure I use after a real conversation.

---

# The 3-Part Structure

---

# The Master Template
`based on what you said about [their specific problem],
the highest-leverage move is probably [specific tactic].

I've done this with [X similar companies / specific case].
if you want, I can walk through exactly what I'd do 
in your situation.

20 mins, no pitch. worth jumping on?`

---

# Why Every Phrase Works
→ "based on what you said" — proves you listened, anchors the call to THEIR problem
→ "the highest-leverage move is probably [X]" — gives free value, proves competence
→ "I've done this with..." — credibility without bragging
→ "walk through what I'd do" — personal, specific, not a sales pitch
→ "20 mins" — low commitment, easier yes
→ "no pitch" — lowers their guard
→ "worth jumping on?" — makes THEM decide, not you push

# What NOT to Do
❌ "Let me show you what we offer"
❌ "I think we'd be a great fit"
❌ "Want to see if we can help?"
❌ "Quick discovery call?"
❌ "I'd love 15 minutes of your time"
All of these center YOU. The soft CTA centers THEM.

---

# The 80/20 of CTAs
The call is about THEM solving THEIR problem.
Not you presenting your offer. Not you pitching your services.
If your CTA can't fit that frame, rewrite it.

# The Notion Pipeline That Tracks Every Lead From Comment To Closed

The biggest mistake I see is people running this whole system out of their LinkedIn inbox.
Then a lead replies on day 4 and they have no idea what was said before.
A simple Notion pipeline solves this in an afternoon.
This is the exact structure I use to track every lead from comment to closed. Build it once, use it for every lead magnet post forever.

---

# The Properties (Database Fields)
Every lead is a row. Every row has these properties:
→ Name (Title)
→ LinkedIn URL (URL)
→ Source post (Select — which lead magnet brought them in)
→ Status (Select — see pipeline below)
→ ICP fit (Select 1-5, from the qualification framework)
→ Last touch (Date — update with every action)
→ Next action (Text — what's the next move)
→ Notes (Text — context, conversation snippets)

---

# The Status Pipeline (8 Stages)

---

# The 3 Views You Need
## 1. Kanban by Status
Drag-drop interface. See every lead's stage at a glance. This is your daily dashboard.
## 2. Table sorted by Last Touch (oldest first)
So nothing falls through the cracks. The lead you haven't messaged in 6 days floats to the top.
## 3. Filter view: "Replied + No call booked + 7+ days"
Your re-engagement queue. Run weekly. These are the leads who showed interest but went cold — most just need ONE more touch to come back.

---

# The Workflow
1. New comment → add row → Status: New
2. Send delivery DM → Status: Delivered
3. They reply → Status: Replied + assign ICP Fit score (use the 10-second framework)
4. Buying signal in conversation → Status: Qualified
5. Calendar booked → Status: Call Booked
6. After call → Status: Proposal or move to Closed Lost
7. Deal closed → Status: Closed Won + add deal value to Notes

---

# Why This Works
You stop trying to remember. You stop scrolling through DMs to figure out where you left off.
Every Monday morning, open the pipeline:
→ Hot leads (Qualified+) get worked first
→ Re-engagement queue gets one touch
→ Long-cold leads get archived
→ New comments from the weekend get loaded in
Most people do this in their head. That's why most people lose leads.
The pipeline isn't fancy. It's the difference between "where did that lead go?" and "they're at stage 4, last touched Tuesday, next action is to send a calendar link."
